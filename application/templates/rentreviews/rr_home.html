Rent Review Process

From 1 May 2026, your landlord will only have the right to increase the rent once each year. However, you will have the right to dispute the increase.


The Rent Review Steps:

To increase the rent the landlord will need to issue you with what is called a Section 13 notice. The landlord must give you at least two months' notice of their intention to increase the rent. Once you receive a Section 13 notice you have two options:

  • Do Nothing: you can simply do nothing, in which case after the two months your rent will automatically increase, or
  • Dispute the Rent: You can dispute the landlords proposed increase, in which case the case will be referred to the First Tier Tribunal (FTT).

If you decide to dispute the landlords proposed rental increase, you must dispute their proposed increase within 2 months' of receiving their notice.


Landlords Issue

The issue most landlords are likely to have is that the onus will be on them to prove that the rent has increased. They will need to provide documentary evidence of comparable transactions for similar properties, collecting this data for most landlords will be challenging and expensive to go through a tribunal process.

On the other hand for the tenant their is no cost to dispute the rent. Even if the landlord does go to the tribunal (and is successful) the rent will only increase from the point the tribunal makes a decision, and the rent cannot be more than the initial Section 13 Notice.


As a consequence of Renters' Rights Act most rent reviews will become a negotiation, where as a tenant you will have considerably more power.

Initiating the Review

  • Either the landlord or tenant submits an application to the First-tier Tribunal to review the rent.
  • The application must specify the proposed new rent and include supporting evidence.

Preparation and Evidence Gathering

  • Both parties gather relevant evidence, such as:
    • Comparable rents for similar properties in the area.
    • Previous rental agreements and history.
    • Market conditions and economic factors affecting rent levels.
  • Each party may submit written representations and evidence to support their proposed rent.

Tribunal Notification and Response

  • The Tribunal acknowledges receipt of the application and schedules a hearing.
  • The other party is notified and given an opportunity to respond or submit additional evidence.

Hearing Process

  • Both parties present their case before the Tribunal, explaining their proposed rent and evidence.
  • The Tribunal questions the parties and reviews evidence presented.

Decision

  • The Tribunal considers all evidence, market data, and circumstances.
  • It makes a binding determination of the new, fair rent.
  • The decision is issued in writing, specifying the adjusted rent and reasoning.

Implementation

  • The new rent amount becomes effective from the specified date.
  • Both parties must adhere to the Tribunal's decision.

Appeal (if applicable)

  • Either party can appeal the Tribunal's decision on specific grounds within a set timeframe.

This process ensures a fair, evidence-based approach to rent adjustments, balancing the interests of both landlords and tenants.
