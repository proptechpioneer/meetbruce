The Renters' Rights Act: What It Means for Tenants

Understanding your new rights from 1 May 2026

The Renters' Rights Act 2025 is described as the "biggest shake-up to renting in England for more than 40 years".

The first phase of the Renters' Rights Act (RRA) which become law on 1 May 2026.

For tenants this means they will have far greater security, greater rights to fight rent increases and challenge landlords with respect to poor housing standards without the fear of being evicted.

Why the Renters' Rights Act Is Good News for Tenants

For years, many tenants have faced uncertainty due to insecure tenancies, unfair evictions, unpredictable rent increases, and discrimination. The Renters' Rights Act addresses these issues head-on, giving tenants greater stability, fairness, and protection.

The Renters' Rights Act will be implemented in three phases from 1 May 2026.

Phase 1: Immediate Improvements from 1 May 2026

Fixed Tenancy Periods come to an End

From 1 May 2026, your tenancy will automatically become a periodic tenancy (regardless of how long is remaining on your tenancy at the time). This gives you the right to stay in your home as long as you wish, unless the landlord has a valid reason to end the tenancy (see below).

However, you can give the landlords just two months' notice to leave the property at any time. Irrespective of how long is remaining on your fixed-term tenancy (post 1 May 2026).

End to 'No Fault' Evictions

Your landlord will no longer be able to end your tenancy without a valid reason. Until 1 May 2026 your landlord will still be able to issue you with a Section 21 Notice often referred to as a 'no fault eviction', assuming you do not have a fixed term tenancy.

From 1 May 2026 landlords will only be able end your tenancy for specific reasons, typically if they want to sell the property, move in a family member, or if you are in rent arrears.

If the landlord does want to regain they will need to issue you with a Section 8 notice and give you a minimum of 4 months' notice.

Controlled Rent Increases

From 1 May 2026, your landlord will only be able to increase your rent once per year and the landlord must give you at least 2 months' notice.

However, if the rent increase is not considered to be fair market rent you will have the right to dispute the landlords proposed rent increase (this process will be FREE for you).

The rent increase will then be referred to what is called the First Tier Tribunal and they will make a decision as to whether or not the proposed increase is fair.

Even if they do decide in the landlords favour, the rent will only then increase from the point at which the tribunal makes a decision and the rent increase will not be back dated.

Ban on Rent Bidding and Upfront Rent

From 1 May 2026, landlords and estate agents cannot ask for, or accept, more than the advertised rent, and cannot request more than one month's rent in advance. This prevents unfair competition and keeps moving costs manageable.

No Discrimination Against Families or Benefit Recipients

From 1 May 2026, landlords and estate agents can't refuse to let to you, withhold information, or stop you viewing a property because you have children or receive benefits. This opens up more housing opportunities for all.

Right to Request a Pet

Form 1 May 2026, landlords must consider requests to keep a pet and respond within 28 days with a valid reason if they refuse. This means more tenants can enjoy pet ownership.

Stronger Enforcement

From 1 May 2026, local councils will get more powers to enforce standards and penalise bad landlords. Rent repayment orders and penalties will be tougher, helping to drive out rogue practices and improve housing conditions.

These changes come into force on 1 May 2026, except for new investigatory powers for local councils, which begin on 27 December 2025.

Phase 2: Greater Transparency and Redress (From Late 2026)

PRS Database & Landlord Registration

  • All private landlords must register their details and property information in a new database
  • Annual fee with contact details, property addresses, and essential safety information
  • Public access to landlord and property information

PRS Landlord Ombudsman

  • Independent Ombudsman offering free redress
  • Compulsory membership for landlords
  • Easier complaint resolution for tenants

Timeline: These measures will be introduced regionally from late 2026, becoming fully operational by 2028.

Phase 3: Decent Homes for All (Dates to Be Confirmed)

Decent Homes Standard

For the first time, all private rented homes must meet a clear minimum quality standard. Local councils will have new powers to take action if your home falls below these standards, ensuring better living conditions for everyone.

Energy Efficiency

The government plans to require most privately rented homes to achieve at least an EPC C energy rating (or equivalent) by 2030, making homes warmer, greener, and cheaper to run.

Health and Safety

A new review of the Housing Health and Safety Rating System (HHSRS) and the extension of Awaab's Law will set clear timeframes for landlords to fix serious hazards. This empowers tenants to challenge dangerous conditions and ensures prompt repairs.

Note: Consultations on these standards have taken place, and further details—including when the rules will come into force—will be announced soon. The expectation is that landlords will start making improvements as early as possible.

Secondary Legislation and What Happens Next

Some details of the Act will be set out in secondary legislation (additional regulations) over the coming years. This includes the technicalities of how the reforms will be implemented and any exemptions, such as for some student accommodation. The government will share more information as it becomes available, so tenants can stay informed about their rights and protections.

Conclusion: A Fairer Deal for Renters

The Renters' Rights Act represents a major step forward for tenants, offering more security, fairness, and quality in the private rented sector. Whether you're worried about unfair evictions, unpredictable rent hikes, discrimination, or poor housing conditions, the new law is designed to give you more control and peace of mind. As each phase is introduced, tenants will see real improvements in their renting experience—making rented homes better, safer, and fairer for everyone.
